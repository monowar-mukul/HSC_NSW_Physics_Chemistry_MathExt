## Question 1(a) — King Richard III & Looking for Richard
### "Evaluate the role of strategy in *King Richard III* and *Looking for Richard* in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

Strategy — as both subject and method — sits at the heart of the textual relationship between Shakespeare's *King Richard III* and Al Pacino's *Looking for Richard*. Shakespeare presents strategy as the defining characteristic of Richard's subjectivity, while Pacino refracts this through his own documentary investigation of how the play is to be understood, performed and transmitted across time. Together, the texts reveal that strategy is not merely a political tool but an organising principle of self-fashioning, whether that self is a medieval king or a twentieth-century auteur.

Shakespeare's Richard is, above all, a strategist of language. His opening soliloquy — "I am determined to prove a villain / And hate the idle pleasures of these days" — is not confession but performance, spoken directly to the audience in a theatrical conspiracy that implicates us in his scheming. His seduction of Lady Anne over the coffin of the man he murdered is the play's supreme demonstration of verbal strategy: Richard converts her grief into desire through relentless rhetorical manipulation, deploying paradox and flattery to overwhelm her resistance. This scene reveals that Richard's strategy is inseparable from his intelligence; his physical deformity, which marks him out as monstrous in the Elizabethan moral schema, is compensated by an extraordinary cognitive agility. The dramatic irony Shakespeare engineers — the audience always knowing more than those Richard deceives — positions us as both repelled and compelled, evaluating Richard's strategy as simultaneously admirable and abhorrent.

Pacino's *Looking for Richard* engages with this duality directly. His documentary is itself a strategic enterprise: by framing Shakespeare through the lens of American cultural identity, Pacino makes the play accessible without diminishing its complexity. The film's reflexive structure — actors discussing character motivations in rehearsal, intercut with fully costumed performance sequences — mirrors Richard's own layering of performance over strategy. When Pacino interviews members of the public about Shakespeare's relevance and receives blank or dismissive responses, he implicitly argues that the barrier between contemporary audiences and the play is not intellectual but cultural, and that the strategy of demystification is the necessary response.

The textual connection between the two works is most powerfully felt in the shared understanding that strategy requires an audience. Richard's theatricality — "Thus I clothe my naked villainy / With odd old ends stolen out of holy writ" — depends on spectators who are deceived; Pacino's filmmaking depends on spectators who are enlightened. Both composers are keenly aware that meaning is made in the transaction between text and audience, and this shared awareness constitutes the deepest link between the works. Where Shakespeare uses dramatic convention to make Richard's strategy visible to us even as it remains invisible to his victims, Pacino uses documentary form to make the strategies of Shakespearean interpretation visible, turning the act of reading into a subject as compelling as the play itself.

Ultimately, the role of strategy in these texts enhances our appreciation of their connection by revealing that great literature is always in some sense strategic — designed to produce effects, to persuade, to transform. Richard manipulates the court; Pacino manipulates the audience's resistance to Shakespeare; and Shakespeare manipulates theatrical convention to produce a villain we cannot stop watching. The textual conversation between these works is enriched by this layering of strategies, each commenting on the other's methods and illuminating what it means to shape meaning through language and form.

## Question 1(b) — Mrs Dalloway & The Hours
### "Evaluate the role of conformity in *Mrs Dalloway* and *The Hours* in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

Conformity functions in both Virginia Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* and Stephen Daldry's *The Hours* as the force that constrains identity, and the space between social performance and private consciousness as the site where both texts locate their most searching enquiries. The textual connection forged between them through Daldry's cinematic reimagining is deepened by their shared insistence that conformity is not merely imposed from without but internalised — and that the cost of this internalisation is registered most acutely in the lives of women.

Woolf presents conformity through the architecture of Clarissa Dalloway's day. Her party-preparations are both genuinely pleasurable and subtly coercive: she arranges flowers, chooses her dress, assembles the social world that constitutes her identity. Yet the interior monologue constantly undercuts these social performances, revealing an inner life that exceeds and sometimes contradicts what conformity permits her to express. Her memory of kissing Sally Seton — "the most exquisite moment of her whole life" — surfaces as a buried alternative to the life she has chosen with Richard, suggesting that conformity has required the suppression of desire and possibility. The stream-of-consciousness technique is Woolf's formal resistance to the linear, conformist structures of the traditional novel, and this formal radicalism mirrors her thematic concerns.

Daldry translates this into cinematic terms through parallel editing across three time periods. The 1951 housewife Laura Brown reads *Mrs Dalloway* in bed while her husband sleeps, and the act of reading becomes an act of resistance: the novel opens a consciousness that her conformist suburban existence cannot contain. Daldry's use of close-up shots of Laura's face as she reads — tears forming, expression shifting — renders the interior experience that Woolf captured through prose in a purely visual register. The connection between the texts is embodied in this moment: Woolf's novel is shown to be literally transformative, its anti-conformist impulse reaching across decades to unsettle a life constrained by 1950s domesticity.

The Clarissa Vaughan narrative in *The Hours* most directly extends the textual conversation by transposing Woolf's concerns into a contemporary key. Meryl Streep's Clarissa is liberated from the gender conformity of Woolf's period — she is openly in a same-sex relationship — yet still constrained by social expectations of what constitutes a meaningful life. Her party-planning for Richard mirrors Woolf's Clarissa's, but Daldry adds irony through Richard's eventual suicide and his accusation that Clarissa has been "living a life" for appearances. This moment ruptures the surface of social conformity most violently, suggesting that even in the apparently liberated present, performance and authenticity remain in painful tension.

The role of conformity thus enhances our appreciation of the textual conversation by revealing it to be a dialogue across time about the persistence of social constraint. Daldry does not simply adapt Woolf; he argues that the questions *Mrs Dalloway* poses — what does conformity cost? what is suppressed in the name of social belonging? — remain urgently contemporary. The textual connection is one of continuity in crisis: the particular form of conformity changes, but the gap between the social self and the inner life it conceals does not.

## Question 1(c) — The Stranger & The Meursault Investigation
### "Evaluate the role of alienation in *The Stranger* and *The Meursault Investigation* in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

Albert Camus's *The Stranger* and Kamel Daoud's *The Meursault Investigation* are bound together by alienation — but where Camus's Meursault experiences alienation as a philosophical condition of his own absorption, Daoud's Harun experiences it as the product of colonial erasure. The textual conversation between these works is one of the most politically charged in the HSC canon, as Daoud's response illuminates the violence that Camus's text naturalises by centering the Arab victim who is never named.

Meursault's alienation in *The Stranger* is presented as existential rather than social. His famous emotional detachment — his inability to cry at his mother's funeral, his indifference to Marie's love — is rendered by Camus through a flat, declarative prose style that mimics the absurdist worldview: "Mother died today. Or maybe yesterday, I don't know." The present-tense narration and paratactic syntax refuse the causal logic of conventional narrative, enacting Meursault's alienation from the very structures of meaning-making that society requires. His murder of the Arab on the beach is depicted as almost accidental — triggered by heat and light — and the Arab himself remains unnamed, without history or family, a narrative absence that reflects the colonial ideology embedded in Camus's text even as it produces Meursault's most philosophically significant act.

Daoud's *The Meursault Investigation* makes that absence its subject. The narrator Harun is the brother of the murdered man, whom he names Musa — an act of nomination that is itself a political and literary counter-strike against Camus's erasure. Harun's alienation is the inverse of Meursault's: where Meursault is alienated from social convention, Harun is alienated from the very narrative that has claimed his brother's death as its inciting incident. His bitterness is directed not only at the colonial power structure that rendered Arabs invisible but at the text itself: "Your author chose to say nothing about him." This meta-fictional dimension — Harun explicitly addressing Camus's novel — produces the textual conversation's most electric moments, as Daoud refuses the aesthetic detachment that Camus's style performs.

The role of alienation thus enhances appreciation of the textual connection by revealing it to be a debate about whose alienation counts as philosophically significant. Camus's absurdism, however radical in its European context, reproduces a colonial logic in which the Arab other is rendered as landscape rather than subject. Daoud restores this subject by giving him a name, a mother, a brother — and by making Harun's alienation both personal and historical. His prose, unlike Camus's cool detachment, is impassioned and accusatory, its rhetorical heat a formal rejection of Meursault's emotional flatness.

What the textual conversation ultimately reveals is that alienation is never neutral: it is always produced within specific historical and political conditions, and the conditions that produced Meursault's philosophical alienation also produced Musa's erasure. Daoud's text does not simply complement Camus's; it challenges and expands it, and this challenge is what most enriches our appreciation of both works — forcing us to read *The Stranger* with newly troubled eyes.

## Question 1(d) — Donne's Poetry & W;t
### "Evaluate the role of humility in Donne's poetry and *W;t* in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

Humility in John Donne's Holy Sonnets and Margaret Edson's *W;t* is not a passive virtue but a hard-won destination, arrived at only through the surrender of intellectual pride. The textual conversation between Donne's seventeenth-century metaphysical devotion and Edson's late twentieth-century drama is anchored in this shared trajectory: both works chart the movement from the arrogance of the mind toward the vulnerability of the spirit, finding in that movement something close to grace.

Donne's Holy Sonnets are saturated with the tension between intellectual mastery and spiritual inadequacy. The conceits of "Death be not proud" — personifying Death as "slave to fate, chance, kings, and desperate men" — demonstrate a mind that cannot stop performing even as it addresses mortality. The paradox that closes the sonnet — "Death, thou shalt die" — is simultaneously a theological argument and a rhetorical triumph, suggesting that Donne uses wit as both a shield and a weapon against the terror of annihilation. Yet in "If poysonous mineralls," this same intellect confesses its insufficiency before God: "But who am I, that dare dispute with thee / O God?" Here Donne achieves humility through the recognition that his extraordinary mental powers cannot earn salvation — that grace, by definition, is unearned.

Edson's Vivian Bearing begins *W;t* as a figure defined by intellectual pride: a professor of seventeenth-century poetry whose academic rigour is a form of emotional armour. Her opening speech — delivered directly to the audience while attached to an IV drip — is a bravura performance of wit and composure, establishing her as a figure who controls meaning through language just as Donne does. Her scholarly engagement with Donne's punctuation — insisting on the semicolon in "Death be not proud" rather than her own edition's comma — initially seems pedantic but reveals her investment in intellectual precision as a stay against disorder.

The dramatic arc of *W;t* is Vivian's humiliation into humility. The medical institution strips away the social and intellectual structures that have defined her, and the moments of genuine connection — with her nurse Susie, with her former mentor E.M. Ashford — occur not through wit but through its absence. Ashford's reading of *The Runaway Bunny* to the dying Vivian — a children's book substituted for Donne's sonnets — is the play's most quietly devastating image: the highest literary intelligence reduced to the simplest human comfort, and discovering in that simplicity something that scholastic rigour never provided.

The textual conversation is most enriched by the recognition that Donne himself underwent this same movement. His later "Hymne to God my God, in my sicknesse" — written on what he believed to be his deathbed — achieves a humility that his earlier combative sonnets only gesture toward. Here the conceit softens into devotion: "I shall be made thy music." Edson's structural choice to make Vivian a Donne scholar thus creates a precise dramatic irony: she has spent her life studying the poems of humility without understanding what they require. The role of humility in both texts enhances their connection by revealing that the journey from intellectual pride to spiritual openness is not merely a seventeenth-century theological problem but a perennial human one.

## Question 1(e) — Keats' Poetry & Bright Star
### "Evaluate the role of creativity in Keats' poetry and *Bright Star* in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

Creativity in Keats's poetry and Jane Campion's *Bright Star* is rendered as both a transcendent aspiration and a mortal ache — a force that opens the imagination to beauty while simultaneously marking the distance between what can be felt and what can be expressed. The textual conversation between these works is deepened by Campion's decision to focus not on Keats's poems as finished products but on the process of their making, exploring creativity as a lived experience rather than an aesthetic achievement.

Keats's "Ode to a Nightingale" is the canonical expression of his creative paradox. The speaker longs to dissolve into the nightingale's song — "to cease upon the midnight with no pain" — but the imagination that enables this aspiration also reminds him of its impossibility: "the fancy cannot cheat so well / As she is famed to do." The ode enacts its own subject: the poem is a creative act that reaches toward permanence while acknowledging its own transience. The nightingale's song outlasts the individual human life — "Thou wast not born for death, immortal Bird!" — yet the poem itself is the only form of immortality available to the poet. Keats's creativity is thus presented as a response to mortality, a way of making meaning in the face of what "Ode on a Grecian Urn" calls "Cold Pastoral."

Campion translates this dynamic into the domestic world of the Brawne household. Fanny Brawne is positioned not as Keats's muse but as a creative intelligence in her own right — her needlework, her fashion, her emotional sensitivity are presented as forms of making that parallel Keats's poetry. Campion's cinematography establishes this connection formally: the close-up shots of Fanny's embroidery — threads pulled through fabric, patterns emerging — mirror the composition of verse, both activities requiring patience, precision, and imaginative investment. This visual argument challenges a Romantic tradition that figured the female beloved as inspiration rather than creator, and enriches our reading of Keats's poems by restoring Fanny to full creative subjectivity.

The role of creativity in the textual conversation is most significant in the scenes depicting Keats composing. Campion resists the Romantic cliché of inspiration as rapture, instead showing Keats at work — revising, listening, searching. The scene in which he reads "Bright star! would I were steadfast as thou art" to Fanny is rendered not as declamation but as intimacy, the poem becoming a form of communication between two people rather than a monument to solitude. This reading relocates creativity from the isolated genius to the space between persons, suggesting that Keats's greatest poems are love poems in the deepest sense — acts of creative attention directed at a particular human being.

Campion's film ultimately argues that creativity, for Keats, is inseparable from the awareness of its limits. His tuberculosis is not a Romantic decoration but a biological fact that sharpens every creative act: the knowledge that time is short makes the nightingale's song more precious and more painful. The textual conversation between poem and film is enriched by this shared understanding that creativity does not transcend mortality but responds to it — that the poem and the film, like the star of Keats's sonnet, are efforts at steadfastness in a world that does not permit it.

## Question 1(f) — Plath's Poetry & Hughes' Poetry
### "Evaluate the role of reinvention in Plath's poetry and Hughes' poetry in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

The textual conversation between Sylvia Plath's *Ariel* and Ted Hughes's *Birthday Letters* is one of the most intimate and contested in literary history — a dialogue between two versions of a shared life, separated by decades and by death. Reinvention is the concept through which both poets transform biographical experience into mythological and literary form, and it is through evaluating their different reinventive strategies that we come to understand the depth and complexity of their connection.

Plath's reinvention in *Ariel* is radical and self-constitutive. In "Lady Lazarus," the speaker remakes herself through the mythology of resurrection and the Holocaust, claiming a terrifying agency — "Out of the ash / I rise with my red hair / And I eat men like air." The confessional mode is transmuted into something far more violent: this is not self-expression but self-creation, the poetic "I" becoming a figure of inhuman power through the act of writing. Similarly, "Daddy" reinvents the speaker's relationship with her father through the controlling metaphor of Nazi oppression, transforming personal grief and anger into mythological dimensions that exceed biography. The danger of reading these poems purely autobiographically is that it misses their deliberate constructedness: Plath is not reporting experience but reinventing it through extreme rhetorical pressure.

Hughes's *Birthday Letters* performs its own reinvention retrospectively. Published thirty-five years after Plath's death, the collection revisits the shared life from Hughes's perspective, constructing a version of Plath that is sympathetic but also, inevitably, shaped by his own needs. "The Shot" presents Plath as someone whose trajectory toward death was determined before she met Hughes — "You were a wild / White filly" aimed at a target — which reinvents his role in her life as witness rather than agent. This is a self-exculpatory reinvention, and students should evaluate it critically: Hughes's version of events is no more objectively true than Plath's, but it is differently motivated and differently constructed.

The most illuminating connection between the paired collections lies in their mirror poems. Plath's "The Arrival of the Bee Box" and Hughes's "The Bee God" both use the figure of the bees — drawn from their shared beekeeping at Court Green — to explore control, danger and the management of destructive impulse. Plath's speaker vacillates between the desire to release the bees and the temptation to keep them contained: "I am no source of honey / So why should they turn on me?" Hughes's poem reinvents this same scene as a moment of Plath's quasi-religious ecstasy. Reading the two poems together reveals how the same biographical material is differently reinvented according to each poet's psychological and aesthetic needs.

The role of reinvention thus enhances our appreciation of the textual conversation by revealing it as an ongoing negotiation of truth, authority and memory. Neither collection is definitive; together they constitute a dialogue in which neither voice can be silenced and neither version can claim final authority. This is precisely what makes the pairing so intellectually productive: the textual conversation invites us to ask not "what really happened?" but "how does reinvention work, and what does it tell us about the relationship between life, suffering and art?"

## Question 1(g) — The Tempest & Hag-Seed
### "Evaluate the role of artistry in *The Tempest* and *Hag-Seed* in enhancing your appreciation of the textual connections between these texts."

Artistry — the conscious shaping of experience through creative form — is both the subject and the method of Shakespeare's *The Tempest* and Margaret Atwood's *Hag-Seed*. The textual conversation between these works is organised around the figure of the artist as manipulator, and the ethical questions that this manipulation raises: whose interests does art serve? Can creativity redeem loss? And what does the artist surrender when they relinquish control?

Prospero is Shakespeare's most self-conscious artist-figure. His magic is explicitly theatrical — he stages spectacles, scripts confrontations, directs the behaviour of all those on the island — and his famous relinquishment of his powers at the play's close, "I'll break my staff / Bury it certain fathoms in the earth," enacts the ambivalence that surrounds artistic power throughout. The masque he conjures for Ferdinand and Miranda is a celebration of artistry at its most idealised: benevolent, ordered, life-affirming. Yet Caliban's complaint — "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother, / Which thou tak'st from me" — exposes the violence that undergirds Prospero's artistic control. The island is not a blank canvas but an inhabited world, and Prospero's artistry is inseparable from colonial appropriation.

Atwood's Felix Phillips in *Hag-Seed* is a contemporary Prospero whose artistic vision has been thwarted by betrayal. Exiled from the Makeshiweg Theatre Festival, he retreats to teach literacy drama in a correctional facility, staging productions of Shakespeare with inmates. His casting of *The Tempest* with prisoners is itself a form of artistry — a strategic alignment of dramatic roles with the lived experiences of his students, so that the play becomes a mirror for incarceration, loss and the hunger for freedom. Atwood's metafictional layering — a man exiled from art using *The Tempest* to enact his revenge — refracts Shakespeare's themes through a contemporary lens that makes their political dimensions newly legible.

The role of artistry in the textual conversation is most enriched by the parallel treatment of Caliban. Atwood's prisoner-Caliban, 8Handz, is given a voice that Shakespeare's Caliban is not: his hip-hop reworking of "Be not afeard" transforms the play's most eloquent speech into a contemporary idiom that insists on the colonised subject's creative power. This reinvention invites a retrospective rereading of Shakespeare's Caliban as a figure whose creativity Prospero has suppressed rather than absent. Atwood's artistry thus performs what Prospero's cannot: an acknowledgement of the island's prior voice.

The evaluation of artistry across both texts ultimately reveals it as morally ambiguous — capable of liberation and of oppression, of healing and of manipulation. Prospero's epilogue — "But release me from my bands / With the help of your good hands" — positions the audience as the artist's final collaborators, suggesting that art is never complete until received. Felix's staging of *The Tempest* achieves its cathartic purpose when his students genuinely inhabit their roles, discovering in Shakespeare's language something that speaks to their particular experience of confinement and longing. The textual conversation enhances our appreciation by showing that artistry, at its best, is not the exercise of individual genius but an act of collective making — and that this is as true for Shakespeare as it is for Atwood.

## Question 2(a) — Jane Austen, *Emma*
### "In what ways are these closing paragraphs an effective resolution to the narrative tensions explored in Austen's *Emma*?"

The closing paragraphs of *Emma* resolve the novel's central tensions with characteristic Austenian precision — simultaneously satisfying the conventions of comic resolution and quietly subverting them. The extract provided accomplishes what Austen has been working toward for the entire novel: the education of Emma's judgement, the rewarding of genuine social virtue, and the exposure of Mrs Elton's pretension — all in the space of two brief paragraphs.

The tension that most powerfully organises *Emma* is the gap between social performance and moral substance. Emma has spent the novel misreading character — elevating Harriet, misjudging Frank Churchill, condescending to Jane Fairfax — and her growth is measured by the narrowing of this gap. The wedding scene resolves this tension through its deliberate plainness: "very little white satin, very few lace veils; a most pitiful business!" Mrs Elton's complaint is perfectly calibrated as a moral touchstone. Her obsession with "finery or parade" is precisely the social vanity that Emma had to overcome, and by placing Mrs Elton's vulgarity at the resolution, Austen signals that Emma has genuinely arrived at a better set of values. The happiness of the "small band of true friends" who witness the ceremony is contrasted with Mrs Elton's superficial judgment, and the contrast confirms that social virtue — warmth, intimacy, genuine affection — has prevailed over performance.

The resolution of Emma and Mr Knightley's relationship is handled with characteristic Austenian indirection. Mr Woodhouse's consent is secured not by sentiment but by practicality — his fear of burglars is resolved by the presence of "the Mr. Knightleys" — a detail that might seem bathetic but is in fact deeply characteristic of Austen's comic realism. The novel has never idealised marriage as purely romantic; it has insisted on its social and economic dimensions. Mr Woodhouse's capitulation through self-interest rather than disinterested approval of his daughter's happiness is not a flaw in the resolution but a confirmation of Austen's worldview: human motivation is always mixed, and a resolution that pretends otherwise would be sentimental rather than truthful.

The closing line — "the wishes, the hopes, the confidence, the predictions of the small band of true friends who witnessed the ceremony, were fully answered in the perfect happiness of the union" — is grammatically delayed and emotionally restrained, its satisfaction coming through accumulation rather than declaration. This is effective precisely because it mirrors Emma's own development: genuine happiness, Austen suggests, does not announce itself with exclamation marks. The resolution is thus fully earned — not because every tension is dissolved, but because Emma and the reader have learned enough to understand that imperfect happiness in an imperfect social world is the only happiness available, and that this is sufficient.

## Question 2(b) — Charles Dickens, *Great Expectations*
### "In what ways are these closing paragraphs an effective resolution to the narrative tensions explored in Dickens' *Great Expectations*?"

Dickens's revised ending to *Great Expectations* — the version in which Pip and Estella part at Satis House with the ambiguous suggestion that he "saw no shadow of another parting from her" — resolves the novel's tensions not through conventional romantic closure but through something more truthful and more mature: the possibility of understanding without possession.

The extract's most significant moment is Estella's declaration: "I have been bent and broken, but — I hope — into a better shape." The phrase is carefully constructed around hesitation — the dash before "I hope" — suggesting that Estella's transformation, like Pip's, is incomplete and uncertain. This tentativeness is more dramatically effective than confident redemption would be, because the novel has been committed throughout to the proposition that suffering does not automatically ennoble: it can also merely wound. The possibility that being "bent and broken" might produce a better shape resolves the tension between Estella's apparent coldness and the capacity for feeling that Pip has always insisted she possesses, without sentimentalising that resolution.

The tension between Pip's romantic idealism and the social and moral education he has undergone is resolved through the fog imagery of the closing line. The morning mists that had risen when Pip first left the forge — accompanying his departure into the false world of great expectations — are recalled and transformed: "the evening mists were rising now." The parallelism is structurally satisfying, returning the reader to the novel's origin while signalling that the psychological journey is complete. The "evening" mists suggest not decline but the natural close of a long day of experience; the light that "shows to me" the absence of "the shadow of another parting" is tranquil rather than blazing, suggesting a mature acceptance rather than romantic triumph.

The narrative tension between self-deception and self-knowledge — the novel's deepest concern — is resolved in Pip's survival of humiliation. He has been stripped of his fortune, his social pretension and his idolisation of Estella, and what remains is a capacity for honest relationship that was unavailable to him at the novel's opening. Estella's request — "Be as considerate and good to me as you were, and tell me we are friends" — is answered simply: "We are friends." The plainness of this exchange, against the elaborate social performances the novel has depicted, is itself the resolution: Pip and Estella are stripped of all illusion and find, in that stripped state, something genuine. Dickens's closing paragraphs are effective because they honour the complexity of what has been lost while affirming what experience has made possible.

## Question 2(c) — Kazuo Ishiguro, *An Artist of the Floating World*
### "In what ways are these closing paragraphs an effective resolution to the narrative tensions explored in Ishiguro's *An Artist of the Floating World*?"

The closing paragraphs of *An Artist of the Floating World* offer what is, characteristically for Ishiguro, an ambiguous resolution — one that is tonally serene and thematically unsettling in equal measure. Masuji Ono's final observations from his bench, watching the young office workers, appear to offer a reconciliation between past and present, guilt and acceptance; but the limitations of his narration undercut this serenity in ways that demand evaluative scrutiny.

The extract's central tension is between Ono's genuine "gladness" at Japan's recovery and the irresolution of the guilt he has been circling throughout the novel. His reflection that "our nation, it seems, whatever mistakes it may have made in the past, has now another chance to make a better go of things" deploys a characteristic Ishiguro strategy: the passive construction "mistakes it may have made" diffuses personal responsibility into collective abstraction. Ono has been complicit in wartime propaganda, and his art contributed to an ideology that led to catastrophe — yet here, as elsewhere in his narration, he manages to acknowledge this obliquely without fully confronting it. The resolution is psychologically plausible but morally incomplete, and this incompleteness is precisely Ishiguro's point.

The young men laughing on the doorstep — one "with something of the open innocence of a child" — are observed with a nostalgia that Ono explicitly names and then appears to overcome: "I feel a certain nostalgia for the past and the district as it used to be. But to see how our city has been rebuilt...fills me with genuine gladness." The conjunction "But" performs the transition from melancholy to acceptance, but it moves too smoothly: the novel has been at pains to show how much has been lost, and a closing paragraph cannot fully redeem that loss simply by asserting gladness. The image of rebuilding applies both to the physical city and to Ono's self-narrative — both have been reconstructed in ways that make them more liveable, but not necessarily more honest.

What makes the resolution effective, within Ishiguro's terms, is its fidelity to the unreliable narrator's limitations. Ono's serenity is not the novel's truth; it is his truth, and the gap between the two is where Ishiguro's meaning resides. The closing line — "One can only wish these young people well" — is both generous and evasive, the benediction of a man who has reconciled himself to his past without fully accounting for it. This is an effective resolution precisely because it refuses to pretend that the narrative tensions have been dissolved: Ono ends as he began, a figure of dignified self-delusion, and the reader, armed with the whole novel, sees further than he does. Ishiguro's achievement is to make this limitation itself the meaning.

## Question 2(d) — T.S. Eliot, *T.S. Eliot: Selected Poems*
### "In what ways is this closing stanza of *Journey of the Magi* an effective resolution to the poetic tensions explored in Eliot's poetry?"

The closing stanza of *Journey of the Magi* resolves the tensions of Eliot's selected poems not through affirmation but through a suspension between two states — birth and death, old world and new — that mirrors the spiritual condition of the modern subject throughout his work. The Magus's final questions — "were we led all that way for / Birth or Death?" — encapsulate the central tension of Eliot's poetry: the search for renewal in a world whose spiritual resources have been exhausted.

The tension between spiritual longing and modern disillusionment runs through *The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock* — "I have heard the mermaids singing, each to each. / I do not think that they will sing to me" — and *The Hollow Men* — "Between the idea / And the reality / Falls the Shadow." In these poems, transcendence is perpetually deferred, the gap between aspiration and attainment constituting the poem's psychological space. *Journey of the Magi* enacts this same tension through the Magus's retrospective narration: the Birth was witnessed — "We had evidence and no doubt" — yet it brought not joy but agony: "this Birth was / Hard and bitter agony for us, like Death, our death."

The paradox of a birth experienced as death resolves the collection's tensions by giving them theological grounding. Eliot's conversion to Anglo-Catholicism provides the doctrinal context: the Birth of Christ requires the death of the old self, the abandonment of the "old dispensation" with its "alien people clutching their gods." The closing lines — "I should be glad of another death" — are therefore not despairing but aspirational: the Magus has glimpsed the possibility of transformation and now longs for the full death-into-life that Christian conversion promises. Compared to *The Hollow Men*'s despairing "This is the way the world ends / Not with a bang but a whimper," this is a qualified but genuine movement forward.

The resolution is effective because it does not pretend to dissolve the tensions it acknowledges. The Magus returns to his kingdoms "no longer at ease here," a condition he shares with Prufrock, who "will grow old" wearing trousers and walking on the beach, and with the inhabitants of *Preludes*, grinding their mornings to a routine that cannot accommodate vision. The difference is that the Magus has seen something worth being uneasy about. Eliot's poetry is most itself when it holds irresolution open — refusing both despair and easy transcendence — and the closing stanza of *Journey of the Magi* achieves this with extraordinary economy, making it one of the collection's most resonant and most representative moments.

## Question 2(e) — David Malouf, *Earth Hour*
### "In what ways is this closing stanza of *Earth Hour* an effective resolution to the poetic tensions explored in Malouf's poetry?"

The closing stanza of *Earth Hour* resolves the poetic tensions of Malouf's collection by arriving at a vision of human existence that is simultaneously humbling and strangely celebratory — a recognition of human smallness within geological and cosmic time that is offered not as despair but as a form of homecoming. The compressed density of the stanza's imagery draws together the themes of consciousness, nature, mortality and time that animate the collection as a whole.

The tension in Malouf's poetry between the human mind's drive toward mastery and the natural world's indifference to that mastery is present throughout *Radiance*, where the light that "makes all things clear" is also the light that will "undo" human constructions, and in *Aquarius*, where the cosmic scale renders human concerns miniature without rendering them meaningless. *Earth Hour* resolves this tension by naming it directly: "Mind / is the maker, mad for light, for enlightenment, this late admission / of darkness the cost." The word "cost" is crucial — it acknowledges that the human drive toward illumination, toward knowledge and progress, has required the suppression of something, a "darkness" that the hour of the title temporarily restores.

The closing image — "our green accommodating tomb" — is syntactically and semantically the stanza's climax. The earth is simultaneously living ("green"), welcoming ("accommodating") and lethal ("tomb"), a paradox that refuses to resolve into simple elegy or simple celebration. The German *Schatzkammer* — treasure chamber — complicates the tomb image: the earth that receives the dead also preserves what is most precious. Malouf positions human life as both briefly inhabiting and ultimately returning to this "ambulant, airy" space, the world being simultaneously a living thing and a depository of the dead.

The "extended cry of our first coming" — the birth cry that initiated our presence in this world — is recalled at the stanza's end as the measure of our total existence: from that cry to the silence of death, the space we inhabit is the same "green accommodating tomb." This is an effective resolution to the collection's tensions because it neither transcends nor despairs of the human condition but holds it steadily in view. *Towards Midnight* and *A Recollection of Starlings* similarly navigate the threshold between human consciousness and the non-human world; *Earth Hour* brings this navigation to its most philosophically concentrated expression, making the hour of darkness not a loss but an acknowledgement of what we share with every living thing that has preceded us.

## Question 2(f) — Henrik Ibsen, *A Doll's House*
### "In what ways is this closing scene an effective resolution to the dramatic tensions explored in Ibsen's *A Doll's House*?"

The closing scene of *A Doll's House* is one of the most structurally and ideologically decisive endings in dramatic literature. Nora's departure — signalled by the famous slamming door — resolves the play's central tensions not through reconciliation but through rupture, and it is the completeness of this rupture that constitutes the scene's effectiveness as both dramatic resolution and cultural provocation.

The extract enacts the collapse of the domestic ideology that has governed the entire play. Helmer's repeated "Nora! Nora!" — spoken to an empty room — is the final image of a man whose authority has been revealed as entirely dependent on his wife's willingness to perform her assigned role. Throughout the play, the dramatic tension has resided in the gap between the doll's house's cheerful surface — the loving wife, the devoted mother, the trinket-like Nora — and the reality of Nora's intelligence, agency and moral seriousness. The revelation of her forgery, and Helmer's response (prioritising reputation over love), strips away the surface irreversibly.

The staging of the final exchange is theatrically precise. Nora's declarative sentences — "It's forbidden," "Nothing," "I'll take nothing from a stranger" — are in formal contrast to Helmer's questions and conditional clauses. He speaks in subjunctives and hypotheticals ("If we changed," "If we discovered"); she speaks in declaratives and imperatives. This grammatical asymmetry dramatises the power shift: Nora is now the one who controls the terms of the conversation, and Helmer's questions — however desperate — cannot draw her back. The conditional "if we discovered some true relationship" is the play's most poignant moment: the possibility of genuine equality is named at precisely the moment when it is foreclosed.

The "miracle" motif that has organised the play's final act is resolved ambiguously. Nora has stopped "believing in miracles" — the miracle being Helmer's willingness to sacrifice himself for her, which he demonstrably lacks — yet the stage direction notes a "hope" flashing across Helmer's face as he wonders if it might yet happen. This ironic closing — Helmer hoping for the miracle Nora has abandoned — is dramatically effective because it denies the audience the comfort of either simple triumph or simple tragedy. The slamming door that follows is realistic sound, not theatrical metaphor: Ibsen trusts the material world to carry the weight of the play's meaning, and it does.

## Question 2(g) — Dylan Thomas, *Under Milk Wood*
### "In what ways is this closing scene an effective resolution to the dramatic tensions explored in Thomas's *Under Milk Wood*?"

The closing scene of *Under Milk Wood* resolves the play's tensions through a return to darkness and dreaming that mirrors the awakening with which it began — but a darkness now enriched by everything the single Spring day has revealed about the lives of Llareggub's inhabitants. Thomas's resolution is not narrative but lyrical, not the conclusion of a plot but the completion of a cycle, and its effectiveness lies in its fidelity to the play's governing rhythm of consciousness, desire and time.

The extract braids together several of the play's persistent tensions. The relationship between love and loss is most poignantly concentrated in Polly Garter's song: "But I always think as we tumble into bed / Of little Willy Wee who is dead, dead, dead." The present pleasure of physical love is shadowed by the memory of a dead beloved, and the repetition of "dead" at the line's end carries an insistence that is both musical and mournful. This is Thomas at his most characteristic: life and death, desire and grief, held in a single image without resolution, because resolution would falsify the texture of experience.

The Myfanwy Price and Mog Edwards exchange resolves their comic subplot with gentle irony: "Oh, my Mog, I am yours for ever" is spoken as she surveys "her own neat neverdull room which Mr Mog Edwards will never enter," while Edwards hugs "his lovely money to his own heart." The self-delusion of romantic fantasy is rendered tenderly rather than satirically — Thomas does not mock his characters but observes them with an affection that accepts human limitation. The closing of this subplot suggests that the play's inhabitants are not to be judged but to be understood, their dreams and self-deceptions as much a part of Llareggub's life as the wood itself.

The final passage of the Voice's narration resolves the play by returning it to the wood — "a God-built garden," "a greenleaved sermon" — and to the darkness from which the single day emerged. The catalogue of what the wood means to different characters (Mary Ann the Sailors, the farmhands, Eli Jenkins) enacts the play's central conviction: that meaning is communal and multiple, that the same world is differently inhabited by each person within it. Thomas's prose-poem closing — "the suddenly wind-shaken wood springs awake for the second dark time this one Spring day" — is an effective resolution because it completes the circle while affirming that the circle will continue: the wood, like life, will spring awake again.

## Question 2(h) — Edmund de Waal, *The Hare with Amber Eyes*
### "In what ways are these closing paragraphs an effective resolution to the narrative tensions explored in de Waal's *The Hare with Amber Eyes*?"

The closing paragraphs of *The Hare with Amber Eyes* resolve the memoir's central tensions with an image of continuity that is both materially specific and emotionally expansive: the netsuke, displaced, dispersed and returned, begin again. This resolution is effective because it does not pretend to recover what has been lost — the Vienna family, the world destroyed by the Holocaust — but instead affirms the endurance of the objects themselves and the meaning that accumulates around them through touch and transmission.

The narrative tension that de Waal has been negotiating throughout is between the desire to recover the past through the objects and the recognition that the past is irrecoverable. The Ephrussi family's history has been painstakingly reconstructed, but always with the acknowledgment that reconstruction is not restoration: the people are gone, the houses are gone, the world is gone. The closing paragraphs resolve this tension not through false recuperation but through acceptance of continuation. The netsuke do not return the family to life; they testify to what survives when almost everything else does not.

The detail of the children moving the netsuke — "when I next look they have been moved around. A rat, curled up asleep, has been pushed to the front" — is quietly perfect as a resolution. It recapitulates the memoir's core argument: that these objects are alive within human use, that their meaning is generated through handling rather than through preservation behind glass. The unlocked vitrine — "unlocked so that the children can open the door if they wish to" — directly inverts the locked cases that protected the netsuke from the Nazi officers, and this inversion is both a personal choice and a philosophical statement about what objects are for.

The closing sentence — "The netsuke begin again" — is the memoir's most resonant phrase, and its effectiveness as resolution lies in its compression. "Begin" implies not mere continuation but renewed vitality; "again" acknowledges the interruption — the theft, the dispersal, the survival — without allowing it to be the final word. De Waal has traced an object history that is inseparable from a family history and a historical catastrophe; his closing resolution refuses both sentimentality and despair, finding in the particular — a rat netsuke slipped into a pocket on the way to work — a form of persistence that is humble, embodied and enduring.

## Question 2(i) — Vladimir Nabokov, *Speak, Memory*
### "In what ways is this closing paragraph an effective resolution to the narrative tensions explored in Nabokov's *Speak, Memory*?"

The closing paragraph of *Speak, Memory* resolves the memoir's tensions through an image of revelation — the ship's funnel hidden in the landscape and suddenly, irreversibly seen — that enacts Nabokov's central understanding of memory itself: that the past, once truly perceived, "cannot be unseen." The resolution is effective because it embodies, rather than merely describes, the process of mnemonic discovery that the entire memoir has performed.

The tension between loss and recovery — the impossibility of returning to childhood's Russia, the destruction of the Nabokov family world by revolution and exile — has been navigated throughout *Speak, Memory* through the aestheticisation of memory. Nabokov does not mourn directly; he recreates, with extraordinary visual and sensory precision, the world that was lost, arguing implicitly that the act of recreation is itself a form of preservation. The closing paragraph extends this argument by positioning memory as a puzzle — "Find What the Sailor Has Hidden" — in which the solution, once found, permanently reorganises perception.

The "blissful shock" that the child will experience on discovering the ship is also the shock that memory produces when it suddenly brings the past into startling clarity. Nabokov and his wife withhold the revelation deliberately — "we did not immediately point out to our child, so as to enjoy in full the blissful shock" — and this withholding mirrors the memoir's own structure, in which revelations are delayed and prepared for, so that when they arrive they carry the full weight of what has been assembled around them.

The closing image — "the finder cannot unsee once it has been seen" — is an effective resolution because it extends the memoir's reach beyond its own pages. The reader, having accompanied Nabokov through his extraordinary reconstruction of memory, has also "found" something that cannot be unseen: a vision of what the past truly is, not lost but preserved in the amber of precise language. Nabokov's achievement in *Speak, Memory* is to make memory an art form, and the closing paragraph is effective because it demonstrates, in its final image, both the impossibility of recovery and the power of perception to make recovery unnecessary.

## Question 2(k) — Gillian Armstrong, *Unfolding Florence*
### "In what ways is this closing scene an effective resolution to the dramatic tensions explored in Armstrong's *Unfolding Florence*?"

The closing scene of *Unfolding Florence* resolves the documentary's central tensions through a convergence of image, music and voiceover that positions Florence Broadhurst's eventual success not as the conclusion of her story but as its vindication — a retrospective confirmation that her extravagance of self and vision was, all along, the point.

The upbeat jazz music that plays throughout the closing scene is a formal choice freighted with meaning. Jazz — improvisatory, exuberant, structurally unconventional — mirrors Broadhurst's own artistic practice: her wallpaper designs are characterised by bold pattern, vivid colour and an indifference to conservative taste. The music's energy at the close recalls the film's opening energy and encloses Broadhurst's life story within a sonic frame that insists on its joyfulness despite everything — the violence, the obscurity, the years of struggle.

The voiceover — "Great success comes when you least expect it" — resolves the documentary's central tension between Broadhurst's self-belief and the world's long reluctance to confirm it. Throughout the film, Armstrong has staged the tension between Broadhurst's extraordinary confidence in her own vision and the repeated failures, reinventions and opacities that characterised her biography. The closing voiceover refuses to sentimentalise this resolution: "when you least expect it" acknowledges that success is not the inevitable reward of merit but something that arrives on its own terms, in its own time. This is an honest rather than triumphalist conclusion.

The images of Broadhurst laughing in the stills — open-mouthed, unselfconscious, vivid in her red dress — resolve the film's tension between the mythologised public figure and the private person by simply showing her alive, in motion, in pleasure. Armstrong does not explain Broadhurst at the close; she allows her to be seen, which is, finally, what Broadhurst always wanted. The resolution is effective because it honours its subject's terms: a woman who believed in spectacle, colour and unapologetic self-expression is given a closing scene that embodies all three.

## Question 2(l) — William Shakespeare, *King Henry IV Part 1*
### "In what ways is this closing scene an effective resolution to the dramatic tensions explored in Shakespeare's *King Henry IV Part 1*?"

The closing scene of *King Henry IV Part 1* resolves the play's dramatic tensions through the figure of Prince Hal's transformation — now fully public, fully endorsed by the king — while simultaneously opening onto the continuation of the very conflicts it appears to settle. Its effectiveness as a resolution lies in this dual movement: closure achieved and immediately deferred.

The central dramatic tension of the play is the question of Hal's identity and loyalty — whether the Prince of Wales who "wastes his days in taverns" will fulfil the dynastic responsibilities that his position demands. The closing scene resolves this tension through action rather than declaration: Hal's conduct at Shrewsbury — killing Hotspur, saving the king from Douglas — has demonstrated through deed what words could not have established. His request to "dispose of" the captured Douglas, and his gift of that disposition to Lancaster, is a gesture of magnanimous authority that confirms his transformation: "His valours shown upon our crests today / Have taught us how to cherish such high deeds, / Even in the bosom of our adversaries."

This generosity toward Douglas — releasing him "ransomless and free" — is dramatically significant because it precisely echoes the chivalric values that Hotspur embodied throughout the play. Hal has not merely defeated Hotspur but absorbed his best qualities, the lesson Shakespeare's history plays consistently teach: that honour is not the property of any single faction but must be earned and renewed by each generation. The closing scene thus resolves the Hotspur-Hal tension by showing that Hotspur's death has not extinguished his values but transferred them to a more capable steward.

The resolution is complicated by its very last lines: "Rebellion in this land shall lose his sway...And since this business so fair is done, / Let us not leave till all our own be won." The play ends on the prospect of further warfare — Northumberland, Scroop, Glendower, the Earl of March still undefeated. This is structurally appropriate for a history play that is the first part of a continuing narrative, but it is also ideologically significant: the "fair" business of Shrewsbury does not close the question of legitimate authority that the Henry IV plays are exploring. The closing scene is effective because it satisfies the immediate dramatic expectation — Hal has arrived — while reminding us that arrival is never final, and that the maintenance of political order requires perpetual, exhausting effort.

**The Keeper**

She had been inside the clock for so long that she had forgotten what it felt like to be on the other side.

Marguerite pressed her palm flat against the glass. Through it, the city was a grey impression — pigeons startling, umbrellas opening, a man pausing on the bridge to look up at her face, which was not her face at all but the face of the clock, the numerals arranged around her like a language she had stopped being able to read.

VI. XII. III. IX.

She remembered learning Roman numerals at a school desk, the teacher's chalk squeaking. The numbers had seemed ancient then, borrowed from a world of marble and empire. Now they were simply where she lived — between the IV and the V, in the hollow space where the minute hand paused at twenty past, that particular angle of light when the morning came through the glass and turned the whole interior gold.

The mechanism behind her breathed. That was the only word for it: a vast, steady exhalation, the mainspring unwinding its stored tension through brass wheels and escapements, each tooth of each gear clicking forward in its appointed sequence. Time, it turned out, was not invisible. Time was iron and grease and the smell of metal warmed by years of use. Time had a sound — not the tick of a wristwatch but something lower, more patient, a vibration that she felt in her sternum before she heard it with her ears.

She had come up the maintenance ladder on the morning they found her father's notebooks. The notebooks had explained everything about the clock and nothing about him — pages of measurements, gear ratios, the torque required to wind the mechanism in winter when the metal contracted. She had climbed through the trapdoor with no intention of staying, but then she had looked through the glass and seen the city arranged below her, miniature and purposeful, and she had not been able to make herself go back down.

Through the glass, the man on the bridge was still looking up. She raised her hand — not waving, just placing her palm against the cold. He would see, if he looked carefully, a shadow behind the numerals. A shape that was not the mechanism. He would think: a bird has got in. He would look away.

The clock breathed. She breathed with it.

Her father had written, in the last notebook, a single sentence that was not a measurement: *A clock does not tell you what time it is. It shows you where you are inside it.*

She had not understood this until now, until she was standing in the iron interior with the city turning grey below her and the Roman numerals spelling out their patient count, and the light changing — the light always changing — from the white of early morning to the gold of mid-morning to the flat pewter of the clouds rolling in from the west.

She was not keeping the clock. The clock was keeping her.

And for now, she thought — pressing her forehead against the cold glass, watching the bridge empty in the first drops of rain — for now, that was enough.

### Part (b) — Reflection (8 marks)

The central image of the clock face, viewed from inside through its glass, presented two immediate compositional possibilities: time as prison or time as perspective. I chose to pursue both simultaneously through the figure of Marguerite, a woman who has retreated into the clock's interior following her father's death, using the ambiguity of her position — between confinement and shelter — to explore the way grief can reorganise our relationship with time itself.

The image's most striking visual element is the view from behind the clock face — the Roman numerals reversed or seen from within, the iron framework casting dramatic shadows. I incorporated this by having Marguerite name the numerals directly — "VI. XII. III. IX." — using the abbreviated form to suggest her familiarity with them, her having absorbed them as spatial coordinates rather than temporal markers. This defamiliarises the clock: instead of a device for measuring time, it becomes a domestic space, a dwelling, which was the central thematic displacement I wanted to achieve.

I used sensory detail — "the smell of metal warmed by years of use," the mechanism's sound as "a vibration she felt in her sternum" — to render the clock's interior as physically present rather than abstractly symbolic. The image's high-contrast black-and-white quality suggested a world stripped of colour and warmth, which I translated into the prose's restraint: short declarative sentences, minimal adjectival decoration, a palette of grey and gold rather than vivid colour.

The father's notebook functions as the narrative's thematic anchor. His final sentence — "A clock does not tell you what time it is. It shows you where you are inside it" — crystallises the piece's central insight and was shaped by the image's inside-out perspective: the clock as interior rather than external reference point. The closing line — "The clock was keeping her" — inverts the expected relationship between the timekeeper and the kept, suggesting that grief, like time, holds us rather than the reverse.

The man on the bridge serves as a structural counterpoint: the outside world looking up at the clock, unaware of what is inside it. His presence allowed me to explore the image's implied relationship between observer and observed, interior and exterior, without resolving it — the narrative ends on an equilibrium rather than a decisive movement, mirroring the clock's own perpetual suspension between one moment and the next.
