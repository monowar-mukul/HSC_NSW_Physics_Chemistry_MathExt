### Question 1 (3 marks)
**Text 1 — Memoir extract (Tove Jansson, *Notes from an Island*)**
**Explain how Jansson celebrates the process of creativity.**

Jansson celebrates creativity as a fundamentally joyful and purposeful process by presenting it as inseparable from physical engagement with the natural world. Her visceral delight in rock — "prising stones out of the ground, heaving them aside" — frames creativity not as intellectual abstraction but as an embodied, sensory experience. The kinetic energy of this language positions the creative act as something raw and instinctive.

Significantly, Jansson celebrates creativity even when it yields nothing permanent. Her description of building "piers which the sea will carry away next autumn and which I'll rebuild better for the sea to carry away all over again" reveals that the process holds intrinsic value regardless of outcome. The cyclical structure of this sentence enacts the very repetition it describes, suggesting that creativity is its own reward — a continual striving rather than a destination.

Jansson further celebrates creativity through its diversity of form, contrasting her own large-scale stone structures with Tooti's patient carving of "the smallest salt spoon ever made." This juxtaposition honours both the monumental and the minute, suggesting that creative expression is valuable across all scales. The closing line — "Sometimes we build things to be solid and lasting, and sometimes to be beautiful, sometimes both" — affirms that creativity encompasses multiple, equally valid purposes, celebrating its breadth as much as its depth.

### Question 2 (4 marks)
**Text 2 — Prose fiction extract (Elena Ferrante, *The Lying Life of Adults*)**
**How does Ferrante portray the ways that family relationships shape identity?**

Ferrante portrays family relationships as the primary lens through which identity is constructed, contested and ultimately reimagined. The narrator Giovanna has been raised with her parents' values — evidenced by her instinct to judge her relatives' furniture as "crude if not vulgar" and her use of "hypercorrect Italian." These internalised judgements reveal how profoundly her parents have shaped her perception of class, culture and self-worth, even before she has examined them critically.

The character of Vittoria functions as a destabilising force on this constructed identity. Her repeated command — "Look, look, look, she hammered into me" — is both an instruction and an accusation, suggesting that Giovanna's family-imposed identity has produced blind spots, rendering her unable to see experiences that might "disturb" her comfortable sense of self. The aggressive anaphora of "Look" conveys how family narratives can suppress authentic perception, conditioning individuals to filter reality through inherited bias.

The transformative power of Ferrante's portrait lies in the name "Giannina." When Vittoria and the extended family use this diminutive, the narrator experiences a profound shift: "that name assigned to me by Vittoria — Giannina — had miraculously brought forth from my same body another person." The contrast between the formal "Giovanna" (her parents' name for her) and the warm "Giannina" (her extended family's name) externalises the idea that identity is relational — shaped by who names us and how. Ferrante thus suggests that family relationships do not merely influence identity; they actively produce competing versions of the self, and the individual must navigate between them.

### Question 3 (4 marks)
**Text 3 — Poem (Ted Kooser, *A Letter in October*)**
**Analyse how Kooser shares insights about the experience of change.**

Kooser uses the seasonal shift from late summer to autumn as a vehicle for exploring the experience of change as both gradual and suddenly overwhelming — a transformation that occurs imperceptibly until it becomes impossible to ignore. The opening line, "Dawn comes later and later now," establishes change through accumulation: the slow, incremental retreat of light mirrors the way human experience shifts not in dramatic ruptures but through quiet, daily erosion.

The extended contrast between what the speaker "only a month ago" could witness — the doe drinking, the light "sowing reflections," the "garden / of trees that grew as if by magic" — and what he now confronts, his own face "mirrored by darkness, pale and odd," reveals change as a loss of outward focus. The imagery of the past is abundant and magical, characterised by movement and life; the present is stark and inward. Kooser's use of enjambment across these stanzas creates a flowing continuity that enacts the seamless way change overtakes us.

The personification of night — wearing "its thick winter jacket," bridling the doe, bringing "its black horse with harness / that creaked like a cricket" — transforms the abstract force of change into a purposeful agent that acts "while I slept." This underscores a central insight: change often occurs beyond our conscious awareness, and we only register its effects upon waking. The speaker's confrontation at "the waiting window" — where he expected to look out but finds only his own reflection — offers Kooser's most poignant insight: that change can redirect us from engagement with the external world toward an unavoidable reckoning with the self. The final lines — "who only wished to keep looking out, / must now keep looking in" — distil this insight with quiet precision, suggesting that change, however unwelcome, compels a form of self-examination.

### Question 4 (4 marks)
**Text 4 — Prose fiction extract (Barbara Kingsolver, *Prodigal Summer*)**
**How does Kingsolver expand the reader's understanding of the relationship between past and present?**

Kingsolver expands the reader's understanding of the relationship between past and present by presenting them not as separate eras but as layers of a single, continuous experience that collapse into each other through the act of memory. The aged Garnett's sudden realisation — "A surprising fact occurred to Garnett then, for the first time in his nearly eighty years" — positions this collapse as spontaneous and revelatory, suggesting that the past does not remain safely historical but erupts unexpectedly into present consciousness.

The hollow log serves as the central symbol through which Kingsolver articulates this relationship. Once claimed as a boyhood "miracle of nature," the log is retrospectively revealed as the product of his grandfather's failed labour — "miscalculating its size and then having to leave it." This layering of personal memory (childhood play) over historical fact (his grandfather's work) demonstrates that every present experience contains multiple temporal dimensions. The past is never simply past; it is embedded in the physical landscape and in the objects that shape our lives.

Kingsolver further complicates the temporal relationship by showing that the present has material advantages the past lacked — the tractor versus the mule — but that this progress involves genuine loss. The phrase "mules or men" conveys a human intimacy with labour that mechanisation has replaced. Garnett's exasperated argument with his neighbour, that "people keep the customs of their own day and time for good reason," initially seems to dismiss nostalgia, yet the entire passage has demonstrated that he himself is deeply embedded in the past. Kingsolver thus reveals that the relationship between past and present is not one of simple progress but of ongoing, unresolved negotiation — the past persists in shaping how we understand ourselves even as we believe we have moved beyond it.

### Question 5 (5 marks)
**Text 5 — Nonfiction extract (Robert Macfarlane, *Underland: A Deep Time Journey*)**
**Evaluate Macfarlane's representation of the connections between humans and the landscape.**

> **Note:** Text 5 was withheld from the examination booklet due to copyright. The following model answer is based on pp. 3–4 of *Underland* (2019), the passage beginning "The way into the underland is through…" and ending "in the darkness."

Macfarlane's representation of the connections between humans and the landscape in the opening of *Underland* is both intellectually rigorous and deeply affecting, operating simultaneously on geological, mythological and psychological levels. His most significant achievement is the dissolution of the boundary between human interior life and the physical world — a collapse that is not sentimental but grounded in the concept of "deep time."

Macfarlane establishes his central conceit through the idea that the underland is where "we have long placed that which we fear and wish to lose," positioning the subterranean landscape as a projection of human psychology. By reading caves and underground spaces as repositories of grief, danger and cultural memory — from prehistoric burial sites to nuclear waste storage — he argues that the human relationship with the landscape is fundamentally reciprocal: we shape it and it shapes our imagination. This mutual implication elevates the landscape from mere setting to active participant in human experience.

The prose itself performs this connection. Macfarlane's language is dense with geological and organic imagery — "karst," "limestone," "deep time" — yet these technical registers are counterpointed by lyrical passages that render the non-human world with sensory immediacy. This stylistic tension enacts the very relationship he describes: rational scientific understanding and embodied, emotional experience cannot be fully separated when humans confront the natural world.

However, one might question whether Macfarlane's representation risks aestheticising landscapes that are also sites of genuine ecological crisis. His lyrical approach, while compelling, can render the sublime predominantly as a site of personal contemplation rather than collective responsibility. Nevertheless, the depth and originality of his treatment — his insistence that the underground is not simply below us but is constitutive of who we are — makes his representation of human-landscape connections genuinely expansive and enduringly thought-provoking.

### Question 6 (20 marks)
**"Analyse how the representation of particular lives in your prescribed text enriches your understanding of the endurance of the human spirit."**

**Sample response — Prescribed text: George Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four***

> **Note:** This is a model extended response. Adapt the argument, evidence and analytical language to suit your own prescribed text. A model response for *All the Light We Cannot See* and *I Am Malala* follows at the end.

---

George Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four* represents the lives of Winston Smith and Julia within a totalitarian system so pervasive that even thought is policed, rendering the mere act of private feeling a form of defiance. Through these particular lives, Orwell enriches our understanding of the endurance of the human spirit not by presenting triumphant resistance but by interrogating the conditions under which the spirit survives, bends and ultimately breaks — and in doing so, illuminating what is most irreducible in human experience.

Winston's interiority is Orwell's primary vehicle for representing the endurance of the human spirit. In a world governed by doublethink and surveillance, Winston's insistence on private thought — "He was already dead, he reflected" — constitutes a radical act of self-preservation. His purchase of the diary and his decision to write "DOWN WITH BIG BROTHER" are not strategic acts of political resistance but expressions of an inner life that the Party cannot fully colonise. Orwell represents this inner life as the essential core of human identity, enriching our understanding that the spirit's endurance is not always visible or heroic; it may manifest as a whisper in a corner rather than a shout in the square.

The relationship between Winston and Julia deepens this understanding by locating the endurance of the human spirit in bodily desire and interpersonal connection. Their affair is explicitly political precisely because it is personal: Julia's declaration that she has "done it scores of times" is an act of defiance against the Party's attempt to eliminate pleasure and love. Orwell's representation of their meetings in the rented room above Mr Charrington's shop — with its coral paperweight, its mahogany bed, its thrush singing outside — constructs a fragile domestic space that exists in opposition to the grey impoverishment of Party life. The coral paperweight, described as "a little chunk of history that they had forgotten to alter," becomes a symbol of everything the human spirit stubbornly retains: beauty, memory, the particular.

Yet Orwell's most profound and disturbing contribution to our understanding comes through the destruction of Winston's spirit in Room 101. Where many narratives of the human spirit celebrate resilience, Orwell is unflinching in representing its limits. When Winston cries "Do it to Julia!" he does not merely betray her; he capitulates to the annihilation of his own selfhood. The horror of this moment is not that Winston is weak but that the systematic application of absolute power can break anyone. Orwell enriches our understanding precisely by refusing consolation: the human spirit, however enduring, is not invulnerable. The novel's closing image — Winston "loving" Big Brother — deepens our appreciation of the spirit's endurance by showing what its absence looks like.

It is, paradoxically, through this bleak representation that Orwell most powerfully affirms the human spirit's value. The very fact that Winston's destruction feels like a tragedy — that readers mourn his capitulation — confirms that something real and irreplaceable has been lost. The spirit endures in the reader's own refusal to accept the world Orwell depicts as inevitable. *Nineteen Eighty-Four* thus enriches our understanding by extending the endurance of the human spirit beyond the text itself: it becomes a call to vigilance, a reminder that the qualities Winston briefly embodied — curiosity, desire, memory, love — are worth preserving precisely because they can be destroyed.

In representing these particular lives with unflinching precision, Orwell reveals that the endurance of the human spirit is neither guaranteed nor simple. It exists in tension with power, circumstance and human fallibility. The lives of Winston and Julia are not inspiring in the conventional sense; they are instructive — and in that instruction lies their lasting power.

### Alternative Model Response — *I Am Malala* (Malala Yousafzai and Christina Lamb)

Malala Yousafzai and Christina Lamb's *I Am Malala* represents a life shaped by the intersection of personal conviction and political violence, enriching our understanding of the endurance of the human spirit as an act of principled, chosen courage rather than passive survival. Through the particular life of Malala — a girl from the Swat Valley who refused to be silenced — the text reveals that the spirit endures not despite adversity but through a conscious decision to resist it.

The representation of Malala's early life in Mingora establishes the conditions from which her spirit would emerge. Yousafzai's voice is characterised by curiosity and an almost infectious love of learning: school is not merely education but identity. When the Taliban begins closing girls' schools, the loss is rendered personally and specifically — the names of classmates, the texture of the classroom — grounding what might otherwise be a political narrative in irreducibly human experience. This particularity enriches our understanding of the spirit's endurance because it reminds us that what is at stake is not an abstract principle but a specific, lived life.

Malala's anonymous blog for the BBC, written as "Gul Makai," represents the human spirit's capacity to find expression even under suppression. The act of writing — bearing witness to the Taliban's encroachment — is both dangerous and necessary, and Yousafzai represents it as a natural extension of who she is rather than a heroic choice. This normalisation of courage enriches our understanding: the enduring spirit is not always exceptional; it may be the ordinary expression of an extraordinary commitment to one's own humanity.

The shooting on 9 October 2012 and Malala's survival and recovery form the text's spiritual and narrative climax. Yousafzai does not represent her survival as miraculous passivity but as an active continuation of purpose: her return to advocacy, her address to the United Nations, her insistence that "one child, one teacher, one book, one pen can change the world," demonstrate that the human spirit endures not merely biologically but teleologically — oriented toward meaning and action. The text enriches our understanding by showing that endurance, at its most powerful, is transformative rather than merely persistent.

Ultimately, *I Am Malala* represents the endurance of the human spirit as inseparable from community and love. The presence of Malala's father, Ziauddin — who refused to clip her wings, who gave her his name — and the support of her mother and the women of her community remind us that individual resilience is always relationally sustained. The spirit endures not in isolation but in relationship, and it is this insight, rendered through the vivid particularity of Malala's own life, that most enriches our understanding of what it means to be irreducibly human.

**Study Tips for Section II:**
- Always anchor your argument in **specific textual evidence** (quotations, scenes, techniques).
- Use the phrase **"enriches our understanding"** at least twice — mirror the question's language.
- Structure: Introduction (thesis) → 3–4 body paragraphs (each with a distinct, escalating argument) → Conclusion (synthesis).
- Each paragraph should move from **technique → effect → significance** to the central concept (endurance of the human spirit).
- Avoid plot summary — always prioritise **analysis** over description.
